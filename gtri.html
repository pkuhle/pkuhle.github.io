Homodyne Scanning Holography | GTRI

My Intern Project

Background

I worked in GTRI's electro-optical systems lab (EOSL) in the summer of 2021. At the time, I was interested in pursuing a career in quantum computing hardware, so I figured I should learn more about optics. My project was to replicate a form of digital holography called homodyne scanning holography. The goal was to digitally reconstruct an object after being scanned with an interference pattern. 

The Process

I completed this internship right after finishing my freshman year, so I still didn’t really know a lot about anything. Needless to say, learning optics fundamentals was very difficult. I started this process by learning about light waves and how they interfere with one another. I began with the “simplest” scenario: deriving the interference pattern produced by two plane waves crossing at a particular angle. I then moved on to deriving the pattern produced by two spherical wavefronts interfering with each other. I verified my derivations by simulating the interference patterns in MATLAB.

After learning some of the fundamentals, I moved on to my actual project of implementing homodyne scanning holography. What you see when you take a picture with a regular camera is really just a light intensity map. Holography is a form of imaging that records phase, allowing the user to figure out where the light came from. In digital holography, one can take advantage of this and choose what plane to reconstruct the image on.

The setup (more technically, an interferometer) I built was based around a laser split into two beams using a beamsplitter. I changed the phase of one of the beams and recombined the two beams to form Fresnel zone plates (FZPs), concentric circles that change phase as a function of beam radius. Using a motorized mirror controlled by MATLAB code, I steered the beam over a resolution target and recorded the light that passed through it with a photodetector.

The Outcome

The work I did will be used as the basis for a possible LiDAR system functioning off of holography.
